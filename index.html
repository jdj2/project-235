This is the data behind the climb: how fast it happened, the funny thing my Garmin keeps getting wrong about me, and exactly what the goal demands. 497 runs, every one of them counted.

01 — The climb

Race-fitness progression

Every race scored as a VDOT — one running-fitness number, computed straight from the result and comparable across distances, so halves and marathons sit honestly on one line. Higher is fitter. In about two years it's gone from the low 50s to the low 60s — the jump from "fit amateur" toward "elite amateur." The hollow node is the November goal; hover any point for the real chip time.

02 — Man vs. machine

Race Day performance vs. Garmin's Race Prediction

The dashed line is Garmin's own marathon predictor — built from training load alone, with no sense of race-day adrenaline, pacing, or sheer stubbornness. The dots are what I actually ran. Green means I beat the watch's number; red means I left time out there. A watch can measure a lot. It can't measure the part that shows up on race day.

03 — Aerobic efficiency

Easy pace at a fixed heart rate

Same effort, faster splits. Monthly average pace for easy runs in a fixed heart-rate band. A rising line means more speed at the same heartbeat — the cleanest physiological proof that the engine got bigger, not just the legs braver.

04 — Volume

Weekly mileage

Bars are weekly totals; the line is a 4-week rolling average — the build underneath the race line. Volume is the single biggest driver of marathon improvement, and this curve is where the speed comes from.

Every run, every day
